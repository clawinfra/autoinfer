Robert Oppenheimer was born in New York City on April 22, 1904, to Julius Oppenheimer, a wealthy textile importer who had immigrated to the United States from Germany in 1888, and Ella Friedman, a painter. He was educated at the Ethical Culture Fieldston School, where his interest in science began. After graduating in 1921, he entered Harvard College, where he majored in chemistry but also took courses in physics, philosophy, and literature. He graduated summa cum laude in just three years.

In 1925, Oppenheimer sailed for England to study at the Cavendish Laboratory in Cambridge under J.J. Thomson. He found the laboratory work tedious and considered giving up physics entirely. However, he was invited to the University of Göttingen to study theoretical physics under Max Born. At Göttingen, he published numerous papers on quantum mechanics and earned his doctorate in 1927. His contributions during this period included the Born–Oppenheimer approximation, which remains fundamental to molecular physics.

The field of quantum mechanics was rapidly developing during the late 1920s and early 1930s. Oppenheimer made significant contributions to the theory of electrons and positrons, and he predicted the existence of the positron before its experimental discovery by Carl Anderson. He also worked on the theory of cosmic ray showers and the Oppenheimer–Phillips process in nuclear physics.

Oppenheimer returned to the United States and accepted positions at both the California Institute of Technology and the University of California, Berkeley. He built a thriving center of theoretical physics at Berkeley, attracting talented students from across the country. His teaching style was legendary — demanding yet inspiring, and many of his students went on to become leading physicists.

The discovery of nuclear fission in 1938 changed the course of physics forever. When the United States entered World War II, Oppenheimer was recruited to lead the Manhattan Project's weapons laboratory at Los Alamos, New Mexico. Under his leadership, the laboratory grew from a small collection of buildings to a community of thousands. He managed the diverse group of scientists, engineers, and military personnel with remarkable skill, earning the respect and admiration of his colleagues.

The first nuclear weapon was successfully tested on July 16, 1945, at the Trinity site in New Mexico. Oppenheimer later recalled that the sight reminded him of a verse from the Hindu scripture, the Bhagavad Gita: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." The bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki followed shortly thereafter, bringing World War II to an end but also ushering in the atomic age.

After the war, Oppenheimer became a leading voice advocating for international control of nuclear weapons. He served as chairman of the General Advisory Committee of the newly created Atomic Energy Commission and used his influence to oppose the development of the hydrogen bomb. This stance put him at odds with powerful figures in the military and government, particularly Edward Teller and Lewis Strauss.

In 1954, during the height of McCarthyism, Oppenheimer's security clearance was revoked following a controversial hearing. The hearing examined his past associations with Communist Party members and his opposition to the hydrogen bomb. Despite testimony from numerous prominent scientists in his defense, the Atomic Energy Commission voted to deny his clearance.
